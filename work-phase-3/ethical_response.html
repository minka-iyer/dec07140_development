Ethical Response

A. Introduction

The client‚Äôs objective is to support the funder‚Äôs aim of understanding community demographics and preferences to improve future resources and for reporting purposes, which are currently unclear. However, the extent of data collection exceeds what is necessary for a newsletter subscription, raising concerns about privacy, consent, user trust, and data relevance.

B. Ethical Reasoning

Relevant ACS Code Principles

  • 1.2.1(a,e) Public Interest: Professionals should prioritise public interest and respect privacy. Collecting intrusive financial and personal information undermines this.
  • 1.2.2 Quality of Life: Systems should enhance users‚Äô quality of life; excessive data collection causes stress and distrust.
  • 2.1(a) Honesty: Users must receive clear explanations of how their data will be used. Lack of transparency violates this principle.
  • 2.2(a) Trustworthiness: Practitioners must respect confidentiality and data integrity; unjustified sensitive data collection breaches this standard.

Consequences if Ignored

  • Loss of Trust and Credibility: Users may perceive the organisation as exploitative or careless with personal data, leading to hesitation in engaging with the site or unsubscribing altogether.
  • Emotional Harm: Being asked to disclose irrelevant sensitive information (e.g., sexuality, income) may trigger discomfort or feelings of exclusion ‚Äî especially for users from vulnerable or marginalised groups.
  • Privacy Violations and Data Misuse: Without strong governance, sensitive data could be leaked, sold, or repurposed, exposing users to risks like identity theft, profiling, or targeted advertising.
  • Reduced Participation and Accessibility: Intrusive forms discourage sign-ups, limiting the diversity of voices represented in the client‚Äôs user base and creating biased datasets that don‚Äôt reflect the broader community.
  • Legal and Reputational Risks: If users report or publicise their negative experiences, both the client and the funder may face reputational damage, reduced engagement, and potential non-compliance with privacy laws (e.g. APP 3, 6, 11).

C. Alternative Solutions and Safeguards

  • Alternative: Use a simplified newsletter form (name + email only)
    • Pros:
      • Complies with data minimisation principles (ACS 2.2a).
      • Increases user trust and willingness to subscribe.
      • Reduces legal and security risks.
    • Cons:
      • Provides the funder with limited demographic data.
      • May require additional surveys later to gather audience insights ethically.
  • Alternative: Make all sensitive fields optional and clearly explain their purpose
    • Pros:
      • Preserves user autonomy and informed consent.
      • Still allows users who wish to contribute data to do so voluntarily.
    • Cons:
      • Risk of incomplete datasets for the funder‚Äôs reporting purposes.
      • Some users may still feel pressured to fill in optional fields if design isn‚Äôt transparent.
  • Alternative: Conduct a voluntary, separate user insights survey
    • Pros:
      • Separates data collection for research from the newsletter function.
      • Enables clearer communication about purpose and consent.
    • Cons:
      • Adds extra design and administrative effort.
      • Risk of lower participation rates.
  • Safeguard: Implement clear privacy and retention policies
    • Pros:
      • Builds trust, demonstrates compliance, and aligns with ACS honesty and trustworthiness principles.
    • Cons:
      • Requires ongoing maintenance and monitoring of data handling practices.

C. Recommendation

Proceed with the newsletter form but limit data to name, email address, gender, and occupation. This achieves the client‚Äôs goal while maintaining data minimisation and informed consent.

After trust is established‚Äîonce approximately 100 participants have subscribed and the newsletter has been active for several months‚Äîa voluntary, separate survey can collect richer data (e.g., spending habits) with full transparency and explicit consent.

This solution balances user privacy with client objectives, aligning with the ACS principles of Public Interest (1.2.1), Honesty (2.1a), and Trustworthiness (2.2a).

D. Client Perspective

I recommend proceeding with the newsletter form but limiting the data collected to essential details such as name, email address, gender, and occupation. This approach fulfils the client‚Äôs objective of understanding their user base while upholding ethical principles of data minimisation and informed consent.

Once user trust has been established ‚Äì after approximately 100 participants have subscribed and the newsletter has been active for several months ‚Äì a voluntary, separate survey can be distributed. This survey can collect richer demographic and behavioural data (e.g., spending habits) while clearly communicating its purpose and ensuring explicit consent.

This course of action represents the most balanced and ethical option because it aligns with the ACS principles of Public Interest (1.2.1), Honesty (2.1a), and Trustworthiness (2.2a). It allows the client and funder to achieve meaningful insights responsibly, without compromising user privacy or autonomy.
