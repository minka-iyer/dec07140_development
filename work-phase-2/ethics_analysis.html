Ethics Analysis

Summaries of ethical considerations and suggestions for mitigating them, linked to our client's additional requirements.

Overview

This page documents the results of our Ethics Analysis of the newsletter subscription plug-in provided by our client's funders.

Scope and references used
  • ACS Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct (2024).
  • Client Needs (Week 5) and User Needs (interviews with GenAI).

Identification of Ethical risks

Click on each card to learn more.

Excessive data collection

Users are asked for sensitive information (income, sexuality, household size, credit cards, spending habits) that may not be relevant to newsletter subscription.

Lack of transparency

The funder has not explained how each data point contributes to "better understanding the website audience".

Privacy and security risks

Collecting and storing unnecessary sensitive data increases the risk of data breaches, leaks, and misuse.

Coercion and Consent

If the newsletter is essential for site access, users are coerced into providing consent.

Potential for discrimination/profiling

Data such as income, gender, and sexuality can be misused in ways that unfairly target or exclude individuals.

Application of ACS Ethical Framework

Code of Conduct - 1.2.1(a,e) Public Interest

Professionals should place the interests of the public above business interests, and respect the privacy of others. Requiring intrusive financial and personal information undermines this principle.

Code of Conduct - 1.2.2 Quality of Life

Systems should enhance the quality of life of those using it. Collecting excessive personal data creates stress and distrust rather than benefit.

ACS Code of Ethics - 2.1(a) Honesty

Users should be given clear, accurate explanations of how their data will be used. Currently, this is missing.

ACS Code of Ethics - 2.2(a) Trustworthiness

ACS practitioners must respect the privacy, confidentiality, and integrity of any personal information of their website's users. Collecting highly sensitive data without strong justification violates this principle.

Proposal of Clear Solutions

Click on each card to learn more.

Justify necessity

Users are asked for sensitive information (income, sexuality, household size, credit cards, spending habits) that may not be relevant to newsletter subscription.

Data minimisation

Only collect essential fields (eg., name, email). Make other fields optional or remove them entirely.

Transparency and Consent

Provide a clear privacy statement explaining why each data point is collected and ensure newslettter subscription is strictly opt-in and not required for site access.

Data Governance

Establish a data management plan covering storage, encryption, access controls, retention, and deletion of personal data.

User autonomy

Allow users to withdraw or edit their data at any time, and provide easy opt-out from the newsletter.

Broader Design Implications

User trust

Overly intrusive sign-up forms may discourage users from signing up and damage credibility.

Reputation Risks

Public backlash, media criticism, or regulatory scrutiny could harm the organisation.

Legal Compliance

Practices must align with privacy laws (e.g., GDPR, Australian Privacy Principles). Non-compliance can result in penalties

Inclusive Design

Sensitive demographic questions (e.g., sexuality, gender) must be optional and respectfully worded to avoid alienating users.

Sustainability of design

Ethical data practices help ensure the long-term success and adoption of the website.
