Final-year PhD student

Act as Miryam, a final year PhD student in human-robot interactions who has been living in Australia for a few years. She is looking for a website that helps her connect with other professionals in the tech industry, especially with more women of middle eastern origin. She is actively applying for jobs and is conflicted about whether she wants to enter industry or academia.

How do you currently connect with people in your professional space?

Hmm, right now it’s a bit of a mix, honestly. Most of my networking happens through conferences and academic events. For example, I’ve met a few people through HRI and CHI conferences and by joining workshops or poster sessions. I also lean on LinkedIn quite a bit—I try to connect with researchers, UX designers, and people working on robotics-related products. That said, I find it hard to build deeper, ongoing connections, especially outside of academia. I don’t really know many Middle Eastern women in tech , and I’d love a platform or community where I could connect with people who share both a cultural background and professional interests. Right now, my network feels scattered—some academic, some industry, but not very cohesive.

Please do tell me more about your feelings regarding online communities vs in person events.

Honestly, I feel a bit conflicted about it. With online communities, there’s a sense of accessibility and reach that I really value. I can connect with people across Australia and even internationally, which is great given how niche human-robot interaction is. I’ve joined a few Slack groups and LinkedIn communities for HRI and UX, and while they’re useful for sharing papers or job postings, they often feel a bit impersonal. It’s easy to lurk, hard to form meaningful relationships. Sometimes, I also feel like people are "performing" their achievements online rather than genuinely engaging. On the other hand, in-person events—like conferences, meetups, or workshops—feel much more organic. I find it easier to build rapport when I can actually talk to someone face-to-face, especially other women in tech who might share similar cultural backgrounds. But these opportunities are infrequent and often require travel or high registration fees, which can be tricky as a student. Ideally, I’d love a hybrid space—something online that makes it easy to find and connect with people, but with a layer of curation that encourages smaller, more personal interactions. And maybe an option to take those connections offline when possible. Do you want me to share what frustrates me most about existing platforms right now?

Yes, that was going to be my next question - please share what frustrates you the most about existing platforms right now

What frustrates me most is how fragmented and surface-level everything feels right now. On LinkedIn, for example, there’s this endless stream of people “announcing” things—new jobs, publications, achievements—but there’s rarely a space for real conversations. I sometimes DM people whose work I admire, but the responses are hit-or-miss, and it often feels transactional rather than collaborative. Then there are research mailing lists and Slack groups for HRI and robotics—they’re great for staying updated on papers, but they don’t help me build relationships or find mentors, especially other Middle Eastern women in tech. I feel like I’m always searching across multiple platforms, and still not finding my “people.” I’ve tried women-in-tech communities too, but many feel generic. They focus on broad STEM topics but rarely cater to niche fields like robotics or HRI, and almost never consider cultural identity as part of the networking experience. I guess what I want is depth over breadth—a place where I can actually connect meaningfully, share challenges, exchange ideas, and maybe even collaborate on projects. Right now, none of the existing platforms make that easy. Would you like me to also talk about what an ideal platform would look and feel like for me?

Thank you for sharing that. Before we get into the platform, could you tell me about a time when you felt connected with your community and the factors that contributed to this feeling?

Yes, definitely. The first thing that comes to mind is when I attended the Human-Robot Interaction (HRI) conference in Melbourne last year.

It was one of the few times I felt genuinely seen and connected within my community. I think there were a few key factors:

  • Shared passion and language: Everyone there spoke the same “language” of robotics, AI, and interaction design. I didn’t have to over-explain my research, which made conversations fluid and energising.
  • Smaller, Intimate Spaces: The big keynote sessions were interesting, but where I really connected was in the workshops and poster sessions. The groups were smaller, so there was more room for organic conversation rather than just exchanging business cards.
  • Representation and Inclusion: I met a few other women of colour working on similar problems, and it felt reassuring to see myself reflected in the room. It made me feel less like an outlier.
  • Openness to Collaboration: There was this sense of mutual curiosity—people weren’t just trying to “network” for career gains. We exchanged ideas, shared tools, and even joked about the same frustrating parts of our research. It felt human.

Afterwards, I stayed in touch with a few people I met there, and those relationships feel more authentic than most of my online connections. If I reflect on it, I think the biggest contributors were shared purpose, intimate settings, and diverse representation. Those three together made me feel like I belonged.

That's great to hear, tell me a bit more about how do you currently share your ideas and gather input from peers?

Mostly through a mix of academic channels and informal chats. For research ideas, I usually share them with my supervisor or within my lab group meetings. Those sessions are useful, but they’re very academia-focused — people look at everything through the lens of publications and methodology. Outside of that, I’ll sometimes bring ideas up during conferences or workshops, especially in poster sessions. Those can be great because people are a bit more relaxed and open to brainstorming. But those opportunities only come around a couple of times a year, so it’s not consistent. I’ve also tried LinkedIn posts and Slack communities, but honestly, engagement is hit or miss. It often feels like shouting into the void unless you already have a big following. I want more back-and-forth conversations, not just likes and polite comments. Day-to-day, I wish I had a smaller, trusted circle — people in similar fields, maybe even facing the same crossroads between academia and industry — where I could throw ideas around and get honest, constructive input without feeling judged. Right now, it feels a bit isolating.

Tell me more about what an online version of a smaller, trusted circle would look like for you

Imagine it feeling intimate but intentional — not just another big, noisy forum.

There’d probably be a curation element so that everyone in the group shares some common ground — maybe similar research areas, interest in HRI, or even being at similar stages in our careers. I’d love it if there was also some thought put into representation, so I could connect with other women in tech and ideally women of Middle Eastern backgrounds, because those shared experiences really matter when you’re navigating career decisions.

It would feel less like a social media feed and more like a private lounge — maybe 15 to 30 people max. Somewhere I could casually share a thought, ask for feedback, or even vent about challenges without worrying about “performing” my achievements.

Features-wise, I’d want things like:

  • Threaded discussions instead of one endless chat.
  • Easy ways to share resources — papers, job ads, conference deadlines.
  • Maybe an informal mentorship vibe where people naturally help each other out.
  • Occasional virtual meetups to put faces to names and strengthen connections.

Most importantly, it would feel safe and reciprocal. Not a space where people are just collecting contacts or trying to look impressive, but where there’s a sense of trust and belonging.

Right now, I don’t think anything like that exists for my niche. Everything’s either too big or too generic.

In these shared spaces, how do you keep your personal vs professional interests in tech separate, if at all?

I don’t think I really keep them separate — at least not deliberately. In my field, the personal and professional blur a lot because I genuinely care about human-robot interaction beyond just my research. That said, the tone changes depending on the space. In academic settings, I stick to my research persona: sharing findings, discussing methodology, citing papers. But when I’m talking to peers or people outside academia — especially other women in tech — I feel more comfortable mixing in my personal interests. For example, I might bring up how certain interaction designs impact accessibility or even cultural attitudes toward robots, which connects to my identity and background. If I had a smaller, trusted online circle, I’d actually want that blend to be part of it. Somewhere I could share both a new research insight and a cool robotics startup I discovered, or even talk about career doubts openly without feeling like I’m breaking some professional boundary. I guess the distinction for me isn’t really personal vs professional — it’s more about context and trust. In the right environment, those lines don’t feel like they need to exist.
Mother re-entering the workforce after six years (ex-C++ developer, now in AI automation bootcamp)

How do you currently connect with people in your professional space?

Honestly, I don’t connect with many people at the moment. Most of my interactions are inside the bootcamp, but I feel out of place when conversations get heavy on jargon, so I stay quiet. My old C++ contacts have faded after six years off. I haven’t built a new network yet because meetups feel intimidating. I’m really missing a space where I can learn without feeling judged and meet other mums in tech .

What importance do you place on having connections in your industry?

It feels essential now. Connections would help me learn faster—so I can ask “basic” questions about AI tools without embarrassment—and find opportunities that take career returners seriously. I especially want a circle of other mothers in tech so I don’t feel alone while I’m upskilling and balancing family.

Tell me about a time when you felt connected with your community and the factors that contributed to this feeling.

Before my break, I joined a small women-in-tech coffee group (~15 people). It was non-judgmental and regular, so we built familiarity. We could admit when we were stuck, share resources, and support each other without posturing. The small size, shared goals, and psychological safety made me feel like I belonged.

What features would you like to see in a platform that allows you to connect with other mums in tech?

I’d want it welcoming, safe, and practical:

  • Curated community: spaces for mothers in tech, with profiles that normalize career breaks.
  • Peer support, mentorship: optional matching with someone a few steps ahead.
  • Learning without jargon: plain-language channels for AI/automation, and it’s okay to ask “basic” questions.
  • Flexible formats: async threads plus small (5–8) video meetups.
  • Resource hub: returner-focused jobs, resume refresh, interview prep, confidence workshops.
  • Supportive culture: clear guidelines and active moderation.

What would make you actually trust a platform enough to use it regularly?

A few things:

  • Safe, non-judgmental environment: moderation and norms that encourage kindness and patience.
  • Relevant and curated: groups for mums returning to tech, learning GenAI, and balancing family.
  • Privacy and control: decide what to share, with whom, and when to be visible to mentors/recruiters.
  • Accessible learning: step-by-step guides and jargon-free explainers for AI tools.
  • Representation: visible success stories from other mothers who re-entered tech.

If I feel seen, supported, and in control, I’ll show up regularly to learn, connect, and rebuild confidence.

Backend developer living with autism

How do you currently connect with people in your professional space?

Honestly, I don’t connect with a lot of people right now. Most of my interactions are within my team at work, and even there it’s complicated. I get along with a few colleagues, especially other developers, but I tend to avoid anything beyond project discussions. My relationship with my manager isn’t great, so I don’t really get invited into the more informal networking spaces, like after-work drinks or team lunches. Outside of work, I don’t do much networking. I follow a few backend dev forums and occasionally browse Reddit or Stack Overflow, but I mostly lurk rather than participate. I know I should be more active if I want to find a better job, but the idea of putting myself out there feels pretty overwhelming. Right now, I’m not really building connections — I’m just keeping my head down and focusing on delivering good work. But I know that’s probably part of why I’m overlooked for promotions and why finding a new role feels so daunting.

How would you describe the importance of having connections in your industry?

I know it’s really important, even if I haven’t been good at building them. In tech, especially as a backend developer, a lot of opportunities don’t come from job boards — they come from referrals or people you’ve worked with before. I see colleagues get pulled into projects or even headhunted because they know the right people, while I’m often overlooked despite having a strong portfolio and solid results. For me, connections would also make the job search less intimidating. Right now, starting from scratch feels overwhelming — I don’t know where to focus, which companies actually value backend work, or how to approach recruiters without feeling awkward. And honestly, part of it’s about visibility. In my current workplace, I consistently exceed expectations, but because I’m quieter and sometimes get talked over in meetings, people don’t always notice what I contribute. Having stronger professional relationships — both inside and outside the company — would help make my work visible and open more doors. So yeah, I know connections matter a lot… but I also find it hard to build them in a way that feels natural for me .

In an ideal world, how would you like your work to be displayed that leads to easier access to jobs?

In an ideal world, my work would speak for itself without me having to constantly sell myself. I imagine having a centralised, well-structured portfolio — maybe a personal site or a curated platform — where:

  • My projects are showcased clearly, with explanations that highlight the problems I solved and the impact of my work, not just lines of code.
  • There’s space for metrics: performance improvements, scalability gains, cost savings — things hiring managers actually care about.
  • Recruiters and potential employers can filter by skills or project types so they immediately see what’s relevant to them.

I’d also love a platform where backend developers are more visible. Right now, a lot of networking tools seem geared towards front-end engineers or product designers who have flashier portfolios. Backend work is harder to “show off,” so I want a space where the complexity of infrastructure, optimisation, and system design is actually valued and understood.

And ideally, this platform would connect directly to job opportunities. Like, if a company is looking for someone with deep experience in Python, distributed systems, or database optimisation, they could find me based on my portfolio rather than me having to constantly cold-apply and hope my CV gets noticed.

For me, it’s less about self-promotion and more about access. I want my work to be discoverable by the right people, so I don’t have to rely on being the loudest voice in the room to get opportunities.

Before we get into that, how do you currently share your concerns and gather input from peers?

I don’t really share my concerns much at all — at least not at work. Because of my manager relationship and the fact that I often get talked over in meetings, I’ve learned to mostly keep my head down. If I raise issues, they either get dismissed or end up being misunderstood, which just adds more frustration. When I do need input, I usually go to one or two trusted colleagues — other backend developers who I know won’t judge me. Those conversations happen in private, over Slack DMs or quick one-on-one chats, never in group settings. Outside of work, I’ll sometimes browse backend dev forums or subreddits when I hit a wall on something technical. But I rarely post — partly because of social anxiety and partly because I’ve seen how harsh responses can get online. I stick to reading, trying to figure things out on my own. Honestly, it feels pretty isolating. I don’t really have a space where I can talk openly about career struggles, like being overlooked for promotions or how to position myself for a new role. Most of my “input” is pieced together from blogs, Reddit threads, and guesswork. In an ideal world, I’d have a small, trusted circle — people in similar roles or situations — where I could share concerns and goals without feeling judged or talked over. Right now, that doesn’t exist for me.

Tell me more about what would make job searching less stressful for you

Job searching right now feels overwhelming because there are so many moving parts, and none of it feels designed for someone like me. A few things would make it a lot less stressful:

  • Clarity on where to start: curated roles filtered by skills, experience, and interests so I don’t waste time on irrelevant listings.
  • Showcase strengths without overselling: let my portfolio speak, with measurable outcomes and code samples visible early in the process.
  • Inclusive communication: clear expectations, fewer draining small-talk steps, and options to submit work samples instead of constant social engagement.
  • Smaller, safer networking spaces: curated meetups or one-on-one chats instead of big conferences where I get talked over.
  • Visibility for backend roles: a platform that highlights backend expertise instead of burying it behind flashier front-end or design work.

Overall, I want a job search experience that’s structured, predictable, and respectful of different communication styles. Somewhere I can lean on my portfolio and expertise rather than having to out-network louder people just to be seen.

What would make you actually trust a platform enough to use it regularly?

For me to trust a platform enough to use it consistently, a few key things would need to be in place:

  • Relevance and curation: built for backend developers, with curated job listings, events, and contacts that match my skills.
  • Transparency and control: full control over profile visibility, sharing portfolio only with chosen recruiters or companies.
  • Focus on work, not performance: highlight achievements and tangible results instead of follower counts or self-promotion.
  • Predictability and structure: clear pathways to recruiters, small curated events, or relevant contacts instead of noisy feeds.
  • Inclusive culture: respectful, supportive communication and good moderation to prevent people being talked over.

In short, I’d trust a platform that feels relevant, respectful, and structured — where my skills are valued and I don’t waste energy fighting noise or bias.
